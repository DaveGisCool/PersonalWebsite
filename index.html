Did someone say coding?

As a young curious kid, I (on several occasions) took apart the family pc. It was a Tandy 386. It was also needed for homework (as well as printing homework to our dot-matix printer) and so it had to be reassembled and working whenver I decided to take it apart. While I fairly quickly figured out what the parts inside did, I was always pretty facinated by how programs actually ran.

So in highschool, I enrolled in my first programming course. PASCAL. This game me a great appreciation for logic and binary pathing. This also helped inspire me to practice a little coding on my graphing calculator (TI-81) and learned how to take input from a user and solve the quadratic equasion with them. Each time you pressed enter on the calculator would show the next step in completing the proof.

This is where I learned that creating something to show others how to do something (what the program was intended for) really was an amazing way to learn something. By the time I got done coding it, I didn't even need the program any more.

Once I got to college, I found out you could make your own web pages using tools and sites online. I would browse some Geocities or Angelfire sites, read their code, copy and paste some of it, check out webmonkey, and eventually I jumbled together enough working stuff to make a horrendous, ugly site! I decided to continue taking programming courses.

I enrolled in FORTRAN, COBOL, Java, C, and C++. While I didn't continue working on any of these languages much past these classes, one of my Communication 200 courses taught us HTML and the beginnings of CSS (this was around 2000). And as part of our final project, I had spoken with the chair of the Ethics department about redesigning their (previously fairly terrible) site. I worked with a couple guys to implement what we knew and worked with the secretary in the office to install DreamWeaver and show her enough to update pages as necessary!
